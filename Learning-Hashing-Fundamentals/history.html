📖 The Story of Hash Functions

From simple checksums to blockchain security - a journey through time

The Early Days of Digital Integrity

In the early 1990s, the internet was growing rapidly. People needed ways to verify files downloaded from bulletin boards and early websites to ensure they weren't corrupted or tampered with.

💾

Historical Context: Dial-up modems, 14.4kbps speeds, and frequent transmission errors made simple checksums insufficient for data integrity.

MD5: The "Quick Fix" That Lasted Too Long

The Story: Ronald Rivest created MD5 in 1992 in just two weeks, as an improvement over MD4, to produce 128-bit fingerprints for files. Fast and simple, it quickly became widely used.

The Fatal Flaw: Collisions

In 2004, researchers generated two different files with the same MD5 hash:

File A (innocent)
Hello, I am a harmless document.
MD5: d41d8cd98f00b204e9800998ecf8427e
File B (malicious)
Warning: Virus detected! (fake)
MD5: d41d8cd98f00b204e9800998ecf8427e

Security Implication: Attackers could create malicious files that appeared legitimate!

SHA-1: The NSA's Standard

The Story: Designed by the NSA in 1995 to replace MD5, SHA-1 produced 160-bit hashes and became the U.S. government standard.

📚

Widespread Adoption: Used in SSL certificates, Git, and various security protocols for more than 20 years.

💥

The Day SHA-1 Broke (Feb 23, 2017): Google researchers demonstrated practical collisions, reducing expected security time from 110 years to just 10 days with modern cloud computing.

SHA-256: Bitcoin's Choice

After SHA-1 weaknesses, the NSA released SHA-2 in 2001, including SHA-256. Longer outputs and stronger security made it suitable for digital currencies like Bitcoin.

₿

Why Bitcoin Chose SHA-256

  • Battle-tested: SHA-256 had been analyzed for 8 years
  • Computationally expensive: Suitable for proof-of-work
  • Secure: No practical attacks known

Bitcoin actually hashes twice: once on the data, then again on the result for extra security.

SHA-3 and Ethereum's Path

SHA-3 (Keccak) was standardized in 2015 after a public NIST competition. Ethereum adopted it for security diversity and future-proofing.

Ξ

Why Ethereum Chose Keccak-256

  • Different design: Sponge construction vs SHA-2 Merkle-Damgård
  • Future-proof: Completely different from SHA-256
  • Security diversity: Ethereum remains secure even if SHA-256 is broken

Note: Ethereum uses Keccak-256, slightly different from the finalized SHA-3 standard.
